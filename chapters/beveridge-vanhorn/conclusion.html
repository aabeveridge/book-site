Conclusion

It is no stretch to say that open access to digital data—of all kinds—is among the most exigent contemporary issues facing digital visual studies, digital rhetoric, and the digital humanities. While we are encouraged by recent efforts to discuss the regulation of social networks among U.S. lawmakers, we fear that the current regulatory climate in Washington D.C. will not lead to the type of accountability that ought to be immediately enacted. In the meantime, we can take action by preserving those datasets that power our own analyses, while also building for the future by establishing data-driven tools and techniques that are designed specifically for digital visual studies, digital rhetoric, and the digital humanities. By so doing, we will not only extend the research capabilities of these fields—if we are brave enough, we can also envision a future where our research takes a serious role in helping to redesign the circulatory systems and networks that are quickly becoming a fundamental component of democracies around the world. The first step is rethinking the business model that funds and sustains these technologies, as well as turning the critique on ourselves to rethink the ways in which our own technophilic tendencies encourage us to quickly adopt technologies that may have disastrous consequences at scale.
